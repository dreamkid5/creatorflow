Imagine you are a king. Not just any king. You rule the most feared army on the face of the earth. Your chariots have crushed every enemy that dared to stand in your way. You have fought the greatest battle in recorded human history. You have signed history's first peace treaty. Pharaohs call you brother. Gods call you their son. And then, almost overnight, everything you built, every palace, every city, every record of your people, vanishes from the earth so completely that for two thousand years, the world forgets you ever existed.

This is not a legend. This is not myth. This is the true and astonishing story of the Hittites. A civilization so powerful it changed the ancient world forever. A people so thoroughly erased by time that even scholars once thought they were fictional. And a mystery so deep that even today, we are still putting the pieces together.

Stay with me. Because what happened to the Hittites is one of the most jaw-dropping stories in all of human history. And by the end of this video, you will understand why their story still matters right now.
The Origins of the Hittites (Before 1650 BC)

Let us start at the beginning. And the beginning is strange.

Around 2000 BC, the land we now call Turkey, which the ancient world knew as Anatolia, was a patchwork of small kingdoms and city-states. Different peoples, different languages, different gods. The land was rugged, full of mountains and valleys, rivers and forests. It was not a friendly place. But it was rich in something the ancient world desperately wanted. Metal. Copper, tin, silver, and iron ore ran through the mountains of Anatolia like veins of gold through bedrock.

Into this land came a people. We are not entirely sure where they came from. Scholars have argued about this for over a century. Some believe they came from the east, from the Caucasus region. Others think they migrated from the Pontic steppe, north of the Black Sea. A few even suggest they were always there, mixed in with the older populations of Anatolia. What we do know is this. They spoke a language called Hittite, which belongs to the Indo-European language family, the same great family of languages that would eventually produce Greek, Latin, Sanskrit, German, and eventually English. Yes. In a very distant way, the Hittites spoke a cousin language to the one you are listening to right now.

They settled in the heart of Anatolia, in a region called Hatti. And here is where things get interesting. When they arrived, there was already a people living there called the Hattians. The newcomers absorbed the Hattians. They adopted their religious rituals, many of their gods, even some of their vocabulary. But they kept their own language, their own identity, and their own fierce ambition.

By around 1800 BC, a network of Assyrian trading colonies dotted the landscape of Anatolia. Merchants from the city of Ashur, in what is now northern Iraq, set up shop in Anatolian cities, trading tin and textiles for silver and gold. These merchants left behind clay tablets, thousands of them, that give us our first real written records of life in early Anatolia. And buried in those records, like a whisper in a crowded room, we find the first references to the Hittite people.

They were not yet an empire. Not yet a power to be feared. But they were coming. And when they arrived on the world stage, they would arrive like a thunderclap.
Pithana, Anitta, and the Birth of Power (Around 1750–1650 BC)

Every empire begins with a single ambitious man. For the Hittites, there were two.

The first was a king named Pithana. He ruled a city called Kussara, a place we have never found. It exists only in ancient texts. Pithana was a conqueror. He swept through central Anatolia, crushing city after city, building a small but formidable kingdom through a simple strategy. Win the battle. Show mercy to the survivors. Let them keep their leaders as long as they paid tribute and stayed loyal. It was a smart approach. It built an empire that did not hate its own master.

But it was his son, Anitta, who truly lit the fire.

Anitta was a man who understood that power is not just about winning. It is about what you do after you win. When he conquered the city of Nesa, also called Kanesh, he made it his capital and declared it sacred. When he crushed a rival king named Piyusti, he did not just defeat him. He destroyed his city, Hattusa, so completely that he cursed the very ground it stood on. He erected a monument declaring that whoever dared to rebuild Hattusa would be struck down by the gods.

That curse would prove famous for one very specific reason. Someone rebuilt Hattusa anyway. And that someone would become the founder of the Hittite Empire itself.

We have an actual tablet recording Anitta's deeds, called the Anitta Text, and it is the oldest known text written in any Indo-European language. Think about that. When you trace all the way back through the history of English, through Shakespeare, through the Normans, through the Anglo-Saxons, through the Romans, through the Greeks, through the mists of ancient history, you arrive at a tablet of baked clay from central Turkey, around 1750 BC, recording the victories of a king named Anitta.

The world was already astonishing before it became the world we know.
Hattusili I and the Empire Takes Shape (Around 1650–1620 BC)

Now here is where our story truly begins. Here is where the Hittite Empire is born. And it is born with a man who defied a curse.

His name was Hattusili. And he rebuilt Hattusa.

Nobody knows exactly who Hattusili was before he became king. He may have been a grandson or great-grandson of Anitta's line. He may have been a regional strongman who seized power through war. The historical record is frustratingly silent on his origins. What we do know is what he did once he had power.

He looked at the ruins of Hattusa, that cursed and barren ground, and he saw something others did not. He saw a fortress. Hattusa sat on a high rocky plateau, easily defended, surrounded by ravines and cliffs. It had access to fresh water. It was centrally located in Anatolia, within reach of multiple trade routes. The curse meant nothing to him. Power meant everything.

He rebuilt the city. He made it his capital. He named himself after it. And then he went to war.

Hattusili I was a military genius. He fought campaigns in every direction. He pushed south into the mountainous region of Kizzuwatna. He fought west against the kingdom of Arzawa. But most remarkably, he pushed his armies south and east, all the way to the great city of Alalakh in northern Syria, and then even further, striking at the ancient city of Ebla. These were not small raids. These were full-scale military campaigns stretching hundreds of miles from home, coordinated across brutal terrain, sustained over years.

And here is something that separates the Hittites from many of their contemporaries. Hattusili wrote about it. He dictated documents describing his campaigns, his victories, his struggles. He wrote in the first person. He says things like, "I, the Great King, Hattusili." He had a sense of himself as a historical figure. He wanted to be remembered.

But Hattusili had a problem. A problem that would haunt the Hittite Empire for its entire existence. Succession. Who would come after him?

He had designated a nephew as his heir, a man named Labarna. But Labarna proved to be a disappointment. He was weak, disloyal, and according to Hattusili's own account, he stirred up trouble, listening to his mother instead of his king. Hattusili was furious. He wrote a document, part political testament, part angry letter, addressed to the assembled nobles and soldiers of his kingdom. In it, he disinherited Labarna completely and named a young boy, possibly his grandson, as his new heir. He named that boy Mursili.

This document is one of the most human pieces of ancient writing ever found. You can feel the anger in it. You can feel an old man's disappointment. You can feel a king who built something great, terrified it will fall apart the moment he closes his eyes.

He was right to be terrified. But not because of Labarna.
Mursili I and the Most Shocking Raid in Ancient History (Around 1620–1590 BC)

Mursili I was young when he became king. But do not let that fool you. He was extraordinary.

He finished what his predecessor started. He consolidated the territories Hattusili had conquered. He fought campaigns in Syria. He was everything a Hittite king was supposed to be. But then he did something nobody expected. Something so bold, so audacious, that historians are still amazed by it over three thousand years later.

He marched his army to Babylon.

Babylon. The greatest city in the world. The city of Hammurabi, the great lawgiver. The city that sat at the heart of Mesopotamia, surrounded by the flat, open floodplain between the Tigris and Euphrates rivers. A city that had never been touched by any outside power in its entire history.

Mursili marched from Hattusa, through the mountains of Anatolia, across the plains of Syria, down the Euphrates River, and struck Babylon like a bolt from a clear sky. The city fell. The king of Babylon, a descendant of Hammurabi's line, was swept aside. The treasuries were emptied. The gods were taken as plunder. The Amorite dynasty that had ruled Babylon for two centuries collapsed in a single afternoon.

And then, as suddenly as they came, the Hittites left.

This is perhaps the most puzzling aspect of the whole campaign. Mursili did not stay. He did not attempt to rule Babylon. He marched his army home, loaded with loot and glory, leaving behind a power vacuum that would be filled by a people called the Kassites. Why did he leave? Was it practical? Was Babylon simply too far to garrison? Was there trouble at home demanding his return? We do not know for certain. But the effect was seismic. The sack of Babylon in roughly 1595 BC reset the entire political order of the ancient Near East.

And then Mursili came home and was murdered.

His own brother-in-law, a man named Hantili, stabbed him to death. We know this because the Hittites wrote it down. They had a remarkable, almost shocking, habit of recording their own failures and crimes in official documents. The murder of Mursili is mentioned frankly in later texts, with no attempt to cover it up.

This began what historians call the dark period of Hittite history. The next hundred years were a nightmare.
The Age of Palace Coups (Around 1590–1450 BC)

What followed Mursili's murder was a parade of horror. King after king was killed, often by the people closest to them.

Hantili, who murdered Mursili, was eventually brought low when his sons were murdered. His successor, Zidanta, killed Hantili's son to take the throne. Zidanta was then killed by his own son, Ammuna. Ammuna's reign brought military disasters. Territories that had been won at such great cost began to slip away. Subject peoples rebelled. The empire shrank.

This pattern, of kings murdered by ambitious relatives, soldiers, or palace officials, was not unique to the Hittites. Courts in the ancient world were vicious places, full of whispers, jealousy, and knives. But the Hittites seemed to suffer from it more than most. The problem was structural. There was no clear law of succession. Any male member of the royal family might have a claim to the throne. Nephews, cousins, brothers, all were potential rivals. And in a world where power was everything, the temptation to act was constant.

The great families of the Hittite court, the panku, a kind of council of nobles and warriors, were supposed to be a check on royal power. In theory, they protected the kingdom from tyranny. In practice, they often drove the very instability that weakened it.

Into this swirling chaos came a man who decided enough was enough. A man who looked at a century of blood and decided to write new rules. His name was Telipinu. And he may have saved the Hittite state from destroying itself entirely.
Telipinu and the Edict That Changed Everything (Around 1525–1500 BC)

Telipinu was king, but not entirely by legitimate means. He had seized power in yet another coup, displacing a previous king. But once in power, he looked at what the Hittite state had become and was appalled.

He issued what we call the Edict of Telipinu, one of the most important documents in ancient history. It was part law code, part political philosophy, part desperate plea to future generations. In it, he laid out the entire history of the Hittite royal family since Pithana, describing the good reigns and the bad ones, the justice and the murders, trying to show through history itself why the kingdom had to change.

Then he laid down the new rules.

First, succession would now follow a specific order. The king's son would inherit first. If there was no son, the king's grandson would inherit. If no grandson, the king's daughter's son would inherit. Only in the most extreme circumstances would someone outside this line be considered. And the choice would be made with proper procedure, not by violence.

Second, and this is remarkable, he placed limits on the punishment of nobles and officials who committed crimes. A guilty official could be fined or stripped of his position. He could not be simply executed at the king's whim, without a proper hearing. Even members of the royal family who committed crimes had certain rights before the court.

Third, he forbade the king from harming the family of a condemned man. If a prince or official was convicted of treason, only that person could be punished. His wife, his children, his servants were to be left in peace.

These ideas, that even kings must follow rules, that justice cannot be arbitrary, that punishment must be proportional, were not common in 1500 BC. In Egypt, the Pharaoh was a god. In Mesopotamia, the king was the gods' representative on earth, his will absolute. The Hittites were doing something genuinely different. They were placing the king inside a legal framework. The king could not be above the law he himself declared.

Scholars have noted this as one of the earliest examples of constitutional thinking in world history. The Edict of Telipinu was not a constitution in the modern sense. But it was a recognition that power must have boundaries. And that recognition, buried in a clay tablet in central Turkey around 1500 BC, echoes down through every later attempt humanity has made to limit the power of those who rule.

Telipinu's edict did not solve everything. The Hittites would have more coups and more instability in the years ahead. But the edict gave them a reference point. A written standard against which kings could be measured and found wanting.
Egypt and the Rise of the New Kingdom (Around 1550–1400 BC)

While the Hittites were sorting out their internal chaos, something enormous was happening in Egypt.

The New Kingdom had begun. A series of brilliant, aggressive pharaohs, Ahmose, Thutmose I, Hatshepsut, Thutmose III, drove Egypt to its greatest power. They pushed south into Nubia. They pushed north into Canaan and Syria. Egypt was suddenly not just a wealthy riverine civilization. It was an empire with ambitions.

This created a collision course. The Hittites controlled Anatolia and parts of northern Syria. Egypt was pushing north through Canaan and wanted Syria too. Between them was a disputed zone, a rich, fertile crescent of land that both great powers considered essential to their security and wealth.

Thutmose III, whom historians sometimes call the Napoleon of Egypt, fought seventeen campaigns in Canaan and Syria over a period of twenty years. He won almost every one. He extended Egyptian power to the Euphrates River, directly bordering Hittite-allied territory. The little kingdoms of Syria, like Mitanni, Kadesh, and Ugarit, found themselves caught between two giants, forced to choose sides, or to play one off against the other for their own survival.

In the middle of all this, the Hittites were also changing.

A new line of kings emerged, beginning with a ruler named Tudhaliya I and continuing through his successors. These kings fought wars to restore Hittite prestige, to recapture territories lost during the long years of instability. They clashed with the kingdom of Mitanni, a powerful state in northern Syria and Mesopotamia that had become a major obstacle to Hittite expansion.

And then came the king who would transform everything.
Suppiluliuma I and the Hittite Golden Age (Around 1344–1322 BC)

His name is one of the longest in ancient history. Suppiluliuma. Say it slowly. Sup-pil-u-liu-ma. Scholars have called him the greatest Hittite king. Some call him the greatest king in the ancient Near East, full stop. That is not an exaggeration.

Suppiluliuma I came to power around 1344 BC, possibly after seizing the throne from his own brother, in true Hittite fashion. Whatever the circumstances of his rise, once in power, he showed abilities that were almost supernatural.

He was a general of breathtaking skill. He was a diplomat of extraordinary shrewdness. He was a builder. He was a legislator. He was a man who understood that an empire is not just won in battle but managed through alliances, marriages, and the careful distribution of power.

His first great achievement was the destruction of Mitanni.

Mitanni had been the dominant power in northern Syria and upper Mesopotamia for generations. It had frustrated Hittite expansion for decades. Suppiluliuma struck it with two great campaigns. He crossed the Euphrates River, something his predecessors had never managed, and drove deep into Mitannian territory. He captured city after city. He installed Hittite-friendly rulers in the Syrian kingdoms that had been under Mitannian influence. Within a decade, Mitanni was finished as a major power. Its territory was split between the Hittites and their new ally, Assyria.

Syria was now largely Hittite. The rich trading cities, Aleppo, Carchemish, Ugarit, all fell under Hittite influence or direct control. The wealth that flowed through those cities, the timber of Lebanon, the metals of Anatolia, the grain of Mesopotamia, the luxury goods of Egypt, now fed the Hittite treasury.

But the most astonishing moment of Suppiluliuma's reign came not from a battle. It came from a letter.
The Most Mysterious Episode in Ancient History (Around 1323 BC)

Tutankhamun was dead.

The boy king of Egypt, that golden, tragic figure, had died young and without an heir. In the chaos of his death, something extraordinary happened. Someone from the Egyptian court sent a secret letter to Suppiluliuma. The letter was from a woman. And she made a request that stunned the Hittite king so thoroughly that he could not believe it was real.

The letter said this, roughly translated: "My husband has died. I have no son. They say you have many sons. Give me one of your sons and he shall become my husband. I will never take a servant of mine and make him my husband. I am afraid."

Read that again. An Egyptian queen, or someone speaking in her name, was asking the Hittite king for a prince. To marry. To become Pharaoh.

This had never happened in Egyptian history. Egypt did not take foreign husbands for its queens. Egypt did not surrender its throne to an outsider. This was unthinkable. Which is exactly why Suppiluliuma did not believe it.

He sent an ambassador to Egypt to investigate. The ambassador returned with a second letter from the queen. She was angry. She wrote that it was humiliating that Suppiluliuma doubted her. She was asking for help. Why would she lie?

Suppiluliuma was convinced. He sent his son, a prince named Zannanza, to Egypt to become Pharaoh.

Zannanza never arrived. He was killed on the road. Murdered. By whom? We do not know. The Egyptians denied any involvement. But the new rulers of Egypt, the general Horemheb and the elderly counselor Ay, had every reason to prevent a Hittite prince from sitting on the throne of the Pharaohs.

Suppiluliuma was devastated. And then he was furious.

He launched a series of punishing raids against Egyptian-held territory in Syria and Canaan. His generals captured thousands of prisoners and brought them back to Hatti. But those prisoners carried something with them. Something invisible. Something far more deadly than any army.

A plague.

The plague swept through Hatti and did not stop for twenty years. Suppiluliuma himself died of it, probably around 1322 BC. His son and successor Arnuwanda II died of the same plague within a year of taking the throne. The golden age had arrived. And it had barely begun before the killing started.
Faith, Fear, and the Voice of a King (Around 1321–1295 BC)

The next king was Mursili II, and he faced a nightmare. Plague was killing his people. Enemies were pressing on every border. His kingdom was on the edge of collapse.

But Mursili II is extraordinary for a reason that has nothing to do with military victory. He wrote prayers. Long, anguished, deeply personal prayers to the gods of Hatti, asking why the plague had come, what the gods wanted, how the wrong could be set right.

These are called the Plague Prayers of Mursili II, and they are among the most moving documents of the ancient world.

In them, Mursili does something almost modern. He examines the past. He goes through historical records, oracle texts, and old documents looking for the sin that caused the gods to punish his people. He finds possible answers. His father had broken a treaty with Egypt. His grandfather had mistreated prisoners. Past kings had not fulfilled their religious obligations. He confesses these sins on behalf of his entire kingdom.

He writes, and I am paraphrasing, "If the plague continues because of the sins of the fathers, what can I do? My father sinned. I did not sin. But the god of the plague is angry, and it is my people dying."

There is something in that line that feels achingly familiar. The idea of inheriting the consequences of what came before. The helplessness of being caught in something larger than yourself. The desperate need to understand why suffering happens to people who do not deserve it. These are not ancient questions. They are permanent ones.

Mursili fought the plague with more than prayer. He was a skilled general, fighting campaigns in Anatolia, Syria, and the western coasts to keep the empire intact while the epidemic raged. He stabilized the kingdom. He survived. And under his reign, despite everything, Hatti held together.
Muwatalli II and the Road to the Greatest Battle (Around 1295–1272 BC)

After Mursili II came his son, Muwatalli II. And Muwatalli looked south and saw something that demanded a response.

Egypt had changed. The weakened and chaotic period following Tutankhamun's death had given way to a new dynasty, the Nineteenth, founded by a general named Ramesses I. His son Seti I was a capable warrior pharaoh. And Seti's son, the man who would come to define Egypt in the modern imagination, was still young. His name was Ramesses. We know him as Ramesses II, or Ramesses the Great.

And Ramesses wanted Syria.

Both sides knew the collision was coming. It was not a matter of if but when and where. Muwatalli spent years preparing. He built alliances with every kingdom and tribe in Anatolia and Syria that could be persuaded to join the Hittite cause. He assembled an army that ancient sources put at over forty thousand men and two thousand chariots, numbers that may be exaggerated but reflect a force that was genuinely enormous by the standards of the age.

Ramesses, meanwhile, marched north with his own massive army, divided into four divisions named after Egyptian gods: Amun, Re, Ptah, and Set. He was confident. He was twenty-five years old, powerful, certain of his own destiny.

The two armies were heading for a place called Kadesh. A fortified city on the Orontes River in what is now Syria. And what happened there, in approximately 1274 BC, would become the most documented battle of the entire ancient world.
The Battle That Shook the World (Around 1274 BC)

What happened at Kadesh is a story of almost catastrophic Egyptian overconfidence, brilliant Hittite deception, and one of the most dramatic near-disasters in military history.

Ramesses marched his army toward Kadesh in four separate divisions, spread out over the landscape to ease the burden of supply and movement. The lead division, the Amun division commanded personally by Ramesses, reached the area near Kadesh first.

There, two local men came to Ramesses with information. They said they were scouts from the Hittite side, defectors, men who wanted to help Egypt. They told Ramesses something wonderful. The Hittite army, they said, was far to the north, near the city of Aleppo, still days away. The road to Kadesh was open.

Ramesses believed them. He pushed forward eagerly, crossing the Orontes River with his Amun division, leaving the other three divisions far behind. He set up camp north of Kadesh and prepared to take the city with little resistance.

He had been completely deceived.

The two men were Hittite spies. The Hittite army was not at Aleppo. It was hidden just east of Kadesh, behind the city walls, thousands of chariots and infantry invisible and silent, waiting for exactly this moment. When two more Egyptian scouts were captured and beaten until they revealed the truth, Ramesses realized with horror what had happened.

The Hittites struck. Two thousand five hundred chariots crashed into the Egyptian Re division, still strung out on the road south of Ramesses, and shredded it. The survivors ran north, screaming, crashing through the camp of Ramesses himself. Suddenly the greatest army Egypt had ever assembled was in chaos. Soldiers running. Officers shouting. And Ramesses, the Pharaoh, the son of Ra, the god-king, was nearly encircled in his own camp.

What happened next is where history and propaganda blur.

In Egyptian accounts, written and carved on temple walls across Egypt for the next sixty years, Ramesses fought with supernatural heroism. Abandoned by his men, he called upon his father, the god Amun. He charged into the surrounding Hittite chariots alone, or nearly alone, and cut through them like a storm. Wave after wave of Hittites he struck down. And they broke.

In Hittite accounts, they won the battle.

Here is the truth, as best as historians can reconstruct it. Ramesses did fight with remarkable personal bravery. His personal guard held the camp long enough for Egyptian reinforcements, a detachment called the Ne'arin, to arrive from the coast and strike the Hittites in the flank. The Hittite chariots, loaded with three men each, were slower and less maneuverable than Egyptian chariots, which carried only two. The Hittite cavalry was caught in a difficult position. They broke and fell back.

But Kadesh itself was never taken. Ramesses could not storm the city. Muwatalli could not destroy the Egyptian army. After two days of fighting, both sides were exhausted, bled, and far from home. A mutual withdrawal was the only logical outcome.

Both sides claimed victory. And in a way, both were right. Neither lost. Neither won. But both sides had looked into the abyss of total war and seen something that frightened them.
The Silver Tablets of Kadesh (Around 1259 BC)

For fifteen years after Kadesh, the two empires continued their rivalry. Skirmishes. Diplomatic maneuvering. Proxy wars through client states. Neither side could land a decisive blow. Both sides had other problems, other enemies, other borders to defend.

Then, in approximately 1259 BC, something historic happened. The two greatest powers in the ancient world sat down and made peace.

We call it the Treaty of Kadesh. The Egyptians called it by one name, the Hittites by another, but the document was essentially the same on both sides. It was written on silver tablets and copies were kept by both parties. The Hittite version was written in Akkadian, the diplomatic language of the ancient world. The Egyptian version was carved into temple walls at Karnak and the Ramesseum.

The terms were remarkable. Both sides agreed to end the war between them. Both sides agreed that if a third party attacked either empire, the other would come to its defense. Both sides agreed on the extradition of political refugees. And both sides called on their respective gods to witness and enforce the agreement.

This document, the Treaty of Kadesh, is the oldest known peace treaty in human history. A copy of it hangs today at the United Nations headquarters in New York, acknowledged as the ancestor of all the peace agreements, all the international treaties, all the diplomatic conventions that have tried, however imperfectly, to regulate the conduct of nations toward each other.

That it was made by a Hittite king and an Egyptian Pharaoh, in a language neither of them spoke as their mother tongue, carved on a metal that no longer exists, three thousand two hundred years before our time, is one of the most quietly extraordinary facts in all of history.

The treaty was sealed, as many ancient treaties were, by a royal marriage. Ramesses II married a Hittite princess, the daughter of the Hittite king Hattusili III, who had by this point succeeded Muwatalli. Letters between the two courts survive. They are warm, almost friendly. Hattusili III and Ramesses II called each other brother. When Hattusili was sick, Ramesses sent Egyptian doctors to help. When Egypt faced a famine, Hatti sent grain.

Two empires that had tried to destroy each other were now, genuinely, at peace.
Life, Culture, and the Way People Lived (Around 1400–1200 BC)

We have spent a great deal of time in palaces and on battlefields. But what was life like for ordinary Hittites? What did this civilization actually look like from the inside?

Hattusa, the capital, has been excavated by German archaeologists since the early twentieth century. What they found was remarkable. The city at its height covered an area of nearly three square miles, surrounded by massive walls of stone and mudbrick. The walls were engineered with great sophistication, featuring multiple towers, postern gates, and a series of interlocking defensive systems.

Inside the walls were temples, palaces, storehouses, and residential areas. The great temple district in the lower city featured a massive temple to the storm god and the sun goddess, the two most important deities in the Hittite pantheon. This temple was not just a place of worship. It was an administrative center, an archive, and a storehouse for royal wealth. Thousands of clay tablets have been found in and around it.

The Hittites were literate, far more so than is sometimes appreciated. The scribal schools of Hattusa trained young men to read and write in cuneiform, the wedge-shaped script of Mesopotamia, which the Hittites had adapted to write their own language. The royal archives contained texts in at least eight different languages, including Hittite, Akkadian, Sumerian, Hurrian, Luwian, and several others. Hattusa was a multilingual, cosmopolitan center of learning and administration.

The Hittites had complex religious lives. They worshipped hundreds of gods, a fact they acknowledged with a phrase that became famous in ancient Near Eastern scholarship. They called their pantheon "the thousand gods of Hatti." These included gods inherited from the old Hattian people, gods absorbed from the Hurrians and Mesopotamians, and local nature deities. The storm god Teshub and the sun goddess Hepat were supreme, but every city, every region, every profession had its own divine protectors.

Religious festivals were elaborate. The king was not just a political ruler. He was the chief priest of the land, responsible for performing dozens of festivals throughout the year to keep the gods happy and the crops growing. A significant portion of the royal archives is taken up with detailed instructions for these rituals, described in such precise and careful language that modern scholars can reconstruct many of them in extraordinary detail.

Hittite society was stratified, like all ancient societies, but it had protections built into its law codes that are surprisingly sophisticated. The Hittite law codes, which survive on tablets, cover everything from murder and theft to property disputes and accidental injury. They often specify not just punishment but also compensation to victims, a concept that feels oddly modern. A man who breaks another man's arm must pay him a certain number of shekels of silver and provide medical care until he is healed. A man who burns another man's orchard must replant it.

The laws show a society grappling seriously with questions of justice, fairness, and human dignity. Not perfectly, not by modern standards. Slaves existed. Women had fewer rights than men. Social rank mattered enormously. But within the context of its time, the Hittite legal system was humane, rational, and sophisticated.
The Hittites and the Metal That Changed the World (Around 1400–1200 BC)

There is one more aspect of the Hittite world that demands attention. Perhaps the most consequential technological fact of their civilization. The Hittites knew how to work iron.

Not just any iron. Smelted, hardened iron. The kind that could be forged into weapons and tools that were stronger, lighter, and more durable than anything bronze could produce.

We have a letter, written by a Hittite king to an Assyrian king, in which the Hittite explains apologetically that he cannot supply the requested iron goods at the moment because production is behind schedule, but he will send what he can. This is astonishing. Iron was something the Hittites were producing on demand, for export, as a luxury material.

The rest of the ancient world was still in the Bronze Age. Bronze was the dominant metal, and its production required specific combinations of copper and tin that were hard to come by. Iron ore, by contrast, is found almost everywhere. But smelting iron requires temperatures much higher than those needed for bronze, and the techniques for achieving and controlling those temperatures were difficult to master.

The Hittites had mastered them. And they guarded the secret jealously. There is evidence that Hittite iron production was tightly controlled by the state, that iron goods were exported selectively, and that the techniques of iron smelting were not shared with the outside world.

When the Hittite Empire collapsed, and we will get to that very soon, those techniques did not disappear. They spread. As Hittite craftsmen and refugees scattered across the ancient world, they carried their knowledge with them. The widespread use of iron that followed, the Iron Age that transformed every civilization that entered it, owes something to the Hittites. That cheap, abundant, iron-based technology eventually allowed poorer peoples to arm themselves as effectively as rich ones, eventually helped undermine the great chariot-based armies of the Bronze Age, and eventually drove the social and economic transformations that shaped the classical world.

The Iron Age started with the Hittites. And the modern world started with the Iron Age.
The Hittite Decline (Around 1250–1200 BC)

Great empires do not die in a day. They fray at the edges, erode from within, and finally crack under a pressure that would not have broken them when they were young and strong.

The decline of the Hittite Empire began slowly after the reign of Hattusili III. His son and successor, Tudhaliya IV, was a capable ruler, but he faced challenges from every direction. The Assyrian Empire, newly energized under an aggressive line of kings, was pushing into areas that the Hittites had long dominated. The western regions of Anatolia were restless, with the kingdom of Arzawa and various other western peoples never entirely submissive. Even within Hatti, there were rival claimants and plots.

Tudhaliya IV responded with building. He oversaw the construction of the great rock sanctuary of Yazilikaya, near Hattusa, where the entire Hittite pantheon was carved in relief into the rock faces of an outdoor gorge. Walking through Yazilikaya, you can still see the gods today, processions of figures in their distinctive Hittite style, pointy hats, upturned shoes, clutching symbols of their power. It is one of the most extraordinary religious monuments of the ancient world.

But temples cannot stop armies. And Assyria was growing.

Tudhaliya IV fought several campaigns against Assyrian expansion, with mixed results. He held on. His successor, Arnuwanda III, had a very short reign. And then came the last great king of Hatti. His name was Suppiluliuma II.

Suppiluliuma II, named after the greatest king in Hittite history, was not without ability. He fought campaigns and won victories. He is the last Hittite king we know of who conducted significant military operations. He fought a naval battle in the eastern Mediterranean, possibly against Sea Peoples, maritime raiders from somewhere in the Aegean or Mediterranean world who were becoming increasingly active.

And then, around 1200 BC, the silence falls.
The End of the Bronze Age (Around 1200 BC)

What happened around 1200 BC is one of the greatest mysteries in archaeological history. Scholars call it the Bronze Age Collapse. And it was catastrophic on a scale almost impossible to comprehend.

Within a period of perhaps fifty years, spanning the late 13th and early 12th centuries BC, virtually every major civilization in the eastern Mediterranean collapsed or severely contracted. The Mycenaean civilization of Greece, the world that produced Homer's Achilles and Agamemnon, disappeared. The great palace culture of Crete was already gone. The thriving city of Ugarit, one of the great trading hubs of the ancient world, was burned and never rebuilt. Cyprus lost most of its major centers. The Egyptian Empire contracted sharply, losing all of its Asian possessions. Mesopotamia went into a long period of fragmentation and weakness.

And Hatti was erased.

The city of Hattusa was burned to the ground. The palace, the temples, the great storehouses, all destroyed by fire. The population scattered. The last tablets found at Hattusa speak of urgent requests for grain, of armies marching to uncertain destinations, of an emergency that was not contained. And then there is nothing.

What caused this collapse? Scholars have argued about it for decades. The best current answer seems to be that it was not any single cause but a combination of catastrophes arriving at the same time, reinforcing each other into a systemic breakdown.

Drought. There is now very strong evidence from climate and geological data that a prolonged drought hit the eastern Mediterranean region beginning around 1200 BC. Crops failed. Populations that depended on the surplus production of fertile regions suddenly faced hunger. Trade networks that had sustained the great palace economies began to break down as goods became scarce.

The Sea Peoples. These mysterious groups, who appear in Egyptian and Hittite records, seem to have been large-scale migrants moving across the Mediterranean, sometimes raiding and destroying, sometimes settling. Who they were exactly is still debated. They may have been displaced populations from the Aegean, themselves driven by drought and political collapse. They attacked coastal cities and may have disrupted the long-distance trade that the Bronze Age economies depended on.

Internal rebellion. Long-running trade networks and palace economies created both wealth and resentment. As systems broke down, the common people who had always lived at the margins may have taken the opportunity to overthrow their overlords.

Earthquake. Some sites in the affected region show earthquake damage at roughly this period.

No single one of these causes could have destroyed everything. Together, arriving in sequence and in combination, they were overwhelming. Civilizations that had seemed permanent simply could not withstand the pressure.

Hatti was gone. Its people dispersed, absorbed, forgotten.
Two Thousand Years of Silence (1200 BC to 1800 AD)

What happened next is almost as remarkable as the civilization itself.

The Hittites were forgotten. Completely. For roughly two thousand years, they existed only as a faint echo. The Hebrew Bible mentions the Hittites dozens of times. They appear as a distinct people in Canaan, as military opponents of Israel, even as marriage partners for King Solomon. Uriah the Hittite is a significant figure in the story of King David. This Biblical evidence convinced many scholars, even in the nineteenth century, that the Hittites had been a real people, but a small one, a minor Canaanite tribe.

Nobody imagined they had once ruled an empire stretching from the Aegean to the Euphrates.

The first clue came in 1834, when a French explorer named Charles Texier stumbled upon the ruins of a massive city near the Turkish village of Bogazkoy. He could not identify it. No ancient city of that size was known in that location. He made careful drawings and notes, but the mystery remained.

In 1879, Irish scholar William Wright noticed that some carved stone figures found in Syria looked different from anything Egyptian or Mesopotamian. He connected these carvings to Biblical references to the Hittites and proposed that they represented a major unknown civilization. He was largely ignored.

In 1880, A.H. Sayce, an Oxford professor, gave a famous lecture arguing that the archaeological evidence pointed to a great Hittite empire. Many colleagues dismissed the idea as absurd. The Biblical Hittites were a small tribe. The idea of a great Hittite empire rivaling Egypt was fantasy.

And then the tablets spoke.

In 1887, Egyptian farmers digging in the ruins of Amarna, the short-lived capital of the pharaoh Akhenaten, found a cache of clay tablets. These were the Amarna Letters, diplomatic correspondence between the Egyptian pharaoh and the kings of neighboring states. Among them were letters from a king called the king of Hatti, in a cuneiform script that nobody could read.

In 1906, German archaeologist Hugo Winckler began excavating the site near Bogazkoy, which he now believed was Hattusa, the Hittite capital. He found thousands of tablets. Thousands. Filling in the history of a great empire that had been invisible for three thousand years.

Bedrich Hrozny, a Czech linguist, cracked the Hittite language in 1915. He recognized it as Indo-European, published his findings, and suddenly the empire had a voice.

The Hittites were back. And everything that had been lost began, slowly and painstakingly, to be recovered.
The Legacy of the Hittites (Ancient World and Beyond)

Empires end. But what they built and what they discovered does not always disappear with them.

The Neo-Hittite kingdoms of Syria and southeastern Turkey survived the collapse of the empire and lasted for another four or five centuries. These were smaller states, ruled by descendants or cultural heirs of the Hittite royal lines, speaking Luwian rather than Hittite, but maintaining many Hittite cultural traditions. Cities like Carchemish, Sam'al, and Malatya continued to flourish under Neo-Hittite rulers until the Assyrian Empire absorbed them in the eighth and seventh centuries BC. Some scholars believe the Biblical Hittites of the time of Solomon and David were these Neo-Hittite people.

The diplomatic innovations of the Hittites outlasted them. The concept of a formal written treaty between sovereign powers, with witnesses, with reciprocal obligations, with provisions for the future, became standard in the ancient Near East after the Treaty of Kadesh. Every later empire in the region, Assyria, Babylon, Persia, inherited this framework.

The Hittite law codes influenced later legal traditions in the region. The idea that punishment should be proportional, that victims deserve compensation, that even kings are bound by law, entered the bloodstream of Near Eastern civilization and did not leave it.

The iron technology that the Hittites pioneered spread after the collapse and drove the Iron Age that followed. Better iron tools meant better agriculture. Better iron weapons meant that armies no longer needed the expensive bronze and the complex trade networks that supplied it. The democratization of military power that iron eventually brought was one of the driving forces behind the rise of citizen armies and, eventually, the political experiments of Greece and Rome that we in the modern world still consider foundational.

And perhaps most importantly, the Hittites remind us of something profound. Civilizations that seem permanent are not. A city that takes a thousand years to build can be reduced to rubble in a season. A language spoken by millions can go silent in a generation. And yet, even in silence, something remains. Written on clay tablets that outlast every archive. Carved in stone that outlasts every palace. Buried in the ground, waiting for a patient hand to find it.
Yazilikaya and the Immortality of the Hittites

Before we close, let us go back to Yazilikaya. That open-air rock sanctuary carved near Hattusa.

If you go to Turkey today and visit the site, you can walk through it. You can stand in the rock cleft and look at the carved procession of gods. Sixty-three deities carved in relief, the entire Hittite divine family assembled for eternity. The storm god Teshub. The sun goddess Hepat. Their son Sharruma. Dozens of lesser gods and goddesses, each carved with their divine symbols.

In a side chamber, there is something different. Twelve warrior gods carved in a row, identical except for their number symbols. These are believed to be associated with the months of the year or with a ritual calendar. And in the center of the chamber is a large carving of the god Sharruma embracing a human figure. That human figure is a king. Some believe it is Tudhaliya IV, the king who built Yazilikaya.

If it is Tudhaliya, then what we are looking at is a king who knew his world was ending. The pressures on Hatti in his reign were immense. Drought, Assyrian aggression, internal unrest. He could not have known exactly what was coming. But he perhaps sensed that the great days were behind him. And so he built this. He carved his gods into the living rock. He made something that could not be burned or stolen. Something that would outlast him.

He was right. Yazilikaya is still there. Tudhaliya is still there, embraced by his god, carved into stone that has endured three thousand years and will endure long after everything we build today has crumbled.

That is the final, stubborn miracle of the Hittites. They left their mark. Deep in the rock, deep in the archives of human history, deep in the frameworks of law and diplomacy and language that still shape our world. They refused, even in extinction, to be entirely gone.
So why does any of this matter? Why should we, living in the twenty-first century, in a world of satellites and smartphones and everything that the ancient world could never have imagined, spend fifty minutes thinking about a civilization that collapsed three thousand years ago?

Because the Hittites teach us things we keep needing to learn.

They teach us that great powers are not permanent. Every empire that has ever existed has believed, at some level, that it would last forever. None of them did.

They teach us that the best weapon is not always a sword. The Treaty of Kadesh, a conversation between two exhausted empires, achieved something that no battle could. Lasting peace, for at least a generation. Diplomacy is not weakness. It is often the highest form of wisdom.

They teach us that the past is not as lost as we think. For two thousand years, the Hittites were invisible. And then a few scholars with a passion for truth and a willingness to look carefully at things other people overlooked, brought them back. History is recoverable. The past speaks, if we are patient enough to listen.

And they teach us, above all, that human beings in every age have faced the same fundamental challenges. How to build something that lasts. How to keep power from corrupting those who hold it. How to make peace with those who have been your enemies. How to endure catastrophe. How to leave something behind worth remembering.

The Hittites tried. They succeeded more than most. And the world they helped shape, through their treaties, their laws, their iron, their languages, their gods carved in stone, is still, quietly, with us.
