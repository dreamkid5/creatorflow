In a wicker basket, splattered with mud and shaking with every blast, there is a single pigeon left alive. A small grey bird with a band around one leg. And the lives of two hundred men now depend on whether that bird can do the one impossible thing being asked of it.

This is the true story of Cher Ami, the pigeon that flew through a wall of fire to save an entire battalion of soldiers. And by the time this bird lands, it will be missing an eye, bleeding from a hole through its chest, and dangling a leg that hangs by nothing more than a thread of tendon.

To understand this story, you have to understand the men who were about to be trapped in that forest.

They came from the streets of New York City. The United States had entered the Great War in 1917, and the Army needed soldiers fast. So they built a division out of the young men of the city, and they called it the Seventy Seventh Division. Some people nicknamed it the Metropolitan Division. Others called it the Liberty Division. But the men themselves had another name for it, a name that told you everything about who they were.

Because that is exactly what it was. In the ranks you had Irish dock workers and Italian tailors. You had Jewish boys from the Lower East Side who had grown up speaking Yiddish at the dinner table. They were not professional soldiers. They were clerks and cab drivers and factory hands. Many of them had never held a rifle before the war. Many had never seen a forest, let alone a battlefield. And now they were being shipped across an ocean to fight in the deadliest war the world had ever seen.

Leading a large group of these men was a tall, thin, quiet officer named Major Charles Whittlesey. And if you were casting a war hero in a film, Whittlesey is not who you would pick. He was a lawyer from Wall Street. He wore round glasses. He was gentle, bookish, and soft spoken, the kind of man who looked like he belonged in a library, not a trench. His own men sometimes joked about how serious and scholarly he seemed.

But underneath that quiet surface was something the men would come to depend on with their lives. Whittlesey did not panic. He did not bluster. He thought carefully, he cared deeply about the men under his command, and once he made a decision, he did not break. That steadiness was about to be tested in a way that no lawyer from Wall Street could ever have imagined.

By the autumn of 1918, the war was reaching its climax. After four long years of slaughter, the Allies had launched a massive offensive to finally break the German army. And one of the most important pieces of that offensive was a dark, tangled stretch of ground in the northeast of France.

A place called the Argonne Forest.

Remember that name. Because the Argonne was about to swallow these men whole.The Argonne Forest was not a gentle woodland. It was a nightmare of a place to fight.

Picture steep ravines and thick, tangled undergrowth. Picture ancient trees packed so close together that daylight barely reached the ground. Picture a landscape carved up by streams and gullies and rocky slopes, where you could not see more than a few yards in any direction. And picture that the Germans had held this ground for four years. Four years to dig in. Four years to build hidden bunkers, string barbed wire, and set up machine gun nests in perfect positions, waiting for anyone foolish enough to come at them.

The Argonne was a fortress made of trees and shadows.

On the first of October, 1918, Major Whittlesey received his orders. His unit, along with several other companies, was to push forward through the forest and take a position along a road near a place called Charlevaux. The plan was that the whole Allied line would advance together, moving forward as one, so that no single unit would be left exposed.

That was the plan. Plans, as every soldier knows, have a way of falling apart.

On the morning of the second of October, Whittlesey led his men forward into the gloom of the forest. They fought their way through the German lines, pushing deeper and deeper into the trees. The going was brutal. Machine gun fire cut through the undergrowth. Men fell. But Whittlesey and his soldiers kept advancing, and by the end of the day they had done exactly what they were ordered to do. They had reached the objective. They dug in on a slope above a marshy ravine, a place that would soon become famous.

There was just one problem.

The units that were supposed to be advancing on their left and right had not made it through. The French troops on one side had been held up. The American units on the other side had been stopped cold by German resistance. Whittlesey and his men had punched a hole straight through the enemy line, deeper than anyone else, and now they stood alone, far ahead of everyone, with open gaps on both flanks.

And the Germans, who knew this forest better than anyone, saw exactly what had happened.

They watched this group of Americans push past them into the trees. And then, quietly, methodically, they closed the door behind them.

By nightfall, Whittlesey's men were surrounded. Roughly five hundred and fifty soldiers, cut off from the rest of the army, trapped in a shallow pocket on a hillside, with the enemy on every side and no easy way back.

The Lost Battalion had just been born.

Now, one thing to clear up. They were not truly lost, and they were not exactly a single battalion. It was a mix of companies from different units all thrown together. And the men always knew roughly where they were. But newspapers back home would soon give them that dramatic name, the Lost Battalion, and it stuck forever. So that is what we will call them too.

The men did not panic that first night. They believed help was coming. They believed the rest of the army would break through and reach them within a day, maybe two. They dug their little holes deeper into the muddy slope, they shared what food they had, and they waited.

They had no idea that they were about to endure five days of hell.
Let me describe the trap they were in, because the geography matters.

The men were dug in on the steep side of a hill, above a boggy stream at the bottom of a ravine. Their pocket was long and narrow, maybe three hundred yards from end to end, and only a short distance across. On the high ground above them, the Germans had machine guns pointing down. On the far side of the ravine, more Germans. Behind them, the way they had come, the Germans had moved back in and sealed the gap. There was no safe direction. Every side was a killing zone.

The soldiers scraped out shallow pits in the earth, what they called foxholes, and pressed themselves as low as they could. To stand up was to invite a bullet. To walk across the open ground was very likely a death sentence. So the men lived in those holes, in the cold and the wet, hour after hour, day after day.

And almost immediately, the supplies began to run out.

They had gone in carrying only what a soldier could carry. A little food. A limited amount of ammunition. A single canteen of water each. Within a day, the food was nearly gone. Men shared crumbs. They chewed on roots and leaves. Within two days, hunger became a constant gnawing companion that never left them.

But hunger was not the worst of it. The worst was the water.

There was water, of course. It was right there at the bottom of the ravine, in the marshy stream. You could see it. But to reach it, a man had to leave his foxhole, crawl or run down the open slope in full view of the German guns, fill his canteen, and somehow get back. The Germans knew the men were thirsty. They knew they would come for the water. So they aimed their machine guns at the stream and simply waited.

Getting a drink of water became an act of suicidal bravery. Some men tried it and were cut down on the slope, their bodies left where they fell because it was too dangerous to retrieve them. The living lay in their holes, throats burning, watching water they could not safely reach.

And then there were the wounded.

As the German attacks came in, more and more men were hit. But there were almost no medical supplies left. The bandages ran out on the first day. So the medics improvised. They tore strips from the clothing of the dead. They packed open wounds with mud and moss to try to slow the bleeding. Men with shattered legs and torn stomachs lay in the open, moaning through the long cold nights, with nothing to ease their pain. There was no morphine. There was nothing.

Through all of this, the Germans kept attacking. They came at the pocket again and again, trying to overrun it, testing the perimeter, probing for a weak point. And each time, Whittlesey's exhausted, starving, freezing men rose up out of their holes and fought them off. Where they found the strength, no one could say. But they held.

And in the middle of all this suffering, Major Whittlesey moved calmly among his men. He kept his composure. He organized the defense. He made sure the perimeter held. He rationed what little there was. And most importantly, he tried, again and again, to get a message out to the rest of the army, to tell them where he was and to beg for help.

Because Whittlesey had one lifeline to the outside world. And it was not a radio. The radios of that era were heavy, unreliable, and easily broken, and his did not work. It was not a telephone, because the wires had been cut. His lifeline was something far older, far stranger, and, as it turned out, far more reliable.

His lifeline had wings.
Here is something that surprises a lot of people about the First World War.

In an age of machine guns, poison gas, tanks, and airplanes, one of the most important tools on the battlefield was a bird. Specifically, the homing pigeon.

For thousands of years, human beings had known about a remarkable ability that these birds possess. If you take a homing pigeon far away from its home loft, into a place it has never been, and then release it, that pigeon will find its way back home. Not sometimes. Almost always. Over enormous distances, through weather, over unfamiliar country, that bird will point itself toward home and fly, fast and straight, until it gets there.

To this day, scientists still argue about exactly how they do it. The birds seem to sense the magnetic field of the Earth. They use the position of the sun. They may even use smell and their memory of landmarks. Whatever the exact method, the result is astonishing. A homing pigeon is a living compass that always points to one place. Home.

Armies had used this ability for centuries to carry messages. And in the First World War, when radios failed and telephone wires were cut and human runners were shot down, the pigeon became a vital link. Each side used tens of thousands of them. A soldier would write a short message on a thin slip of paper, roll it up tight, and slip it into a tiny metal capsule attached to the bird's leg. Then he would release the pigeon, and it would fly back to its loft, often miles behind the lines, where the message would be read and passed up the chain of command.

The United States Army Signal Corps ran its own pigeon service. And among the birds they used were several that had been donated by pigeon breeders in Britain. One of these birds was a black check cock, a grey and dark feathered homing pigeon, that had been given a French name.

Cher Ami.

In French, those two words mean Dear Friend.

For years afterward, people assumed Cher Ami was female. But later examination suggested the bird may in fact have been male. Even the sex of this famous pigeon became a small mystery. But whatever Cher Ami was, one thing is certain. This bird was about to earn that name, Dear Friend, more completely than anyone could have imagined.

Whittlesey's trapped men had brought a number of these pigeons with them into the forest, carried in wicker baskets by a soldier whose job it was to look after them. Cher Ami was among them. And as the days passed and the situation grew more and more desperate, those birds became the only hope of reaching the outside world.

One by one, Whittlesey began to send them.

He would scribble a message describing the situation, roll it into the capsule, and release a bird into the sky above the pocket. And the men would watch it go, watch it circle once to get its bearings, and then streak away over the trees toward home, carrying their desperate words with it.

But the Germans knew about the pigeons too. And they knew what those birds meant. So they had riflemen watching the sky, waiting to shoot down any pigeon that rose from the American position.

Sending a pigeon was a gamble every single time. And on the fourth of October, the men of the Lost Battalion were about to make the most desperate gamble of them all.
By the fourth day in the pocket, the situation had gone from desperate to catastrophic.

The men were starving. Whole companies had been reduced to a handful of survivors. The wounded were dying slowly in the open. Ammunition was running dangerously low, so low that the men were told to hold their fire unless they had a clear target, because every bullet had to count. The Germans, sensing that the Americans were near the end, pressed their attacks harder.

At one point, the Germans even tried a psychological trick. They sent a captured American soldier back into the pocket carrying a note. The note was a demand for surrender, written in polite, formal language. It praised the Americans for their bravery and pointed out, correctly, that their situation was hopeless. It suggested that surrendering would be the honorable and humane thing to do, to spare the lives of the wounded.

The men waited to hear what Major Whittlesey would say.

There is a legend that Whittlesey responded with a short, defiant curse. In truth, he probably did not say anything so dramatic. What he actually did was quieter and, in a way, far more powerful. He simply refused to reply at all. He gave orders to take down some panels that the men had laid out on the ground, panels that were meant to signal their position to friendly aircraft, because he did not want the Germans thinking those panels were any kind of white flag.

The message to his men was unmistakable. There would be no surrender. They would hold. They would fight. And they would wait for the army to break through and reach them.

But hope was running out. And that same day, the fourth of October, the situation was about to become something out of a nightmare.

You see, the rest of the American army had not forgotten the Lost Battalion. Far from it. Commanders knew roughly where Whittlesey's men were trapped, and they were desperate to help. They planned artillery barrages to smash the German positions around the pocket and relieve the pressure. The idea was to rain shells down on the Germans surrounding the Americans, to break the ring of the trap.

But here is the deadly problem with artillery in a forest, when you cannot see your target, and when you are not entirely certain of the exact position of your own men.

You aim by map coordinates. You calculate the range. You fire shells from miles away at a spot on a map. And if that calculation is even slightly off, if the position of your own men is not exactly where you think it is, then those shells do not land on the enemy.

They land on your friends.

And on the afternoon of the fourth of October, that is precisely what began to happen.
It started as a distant sound. The dull thump of guns firing far away, behind the American lines. To the trapped men, that sound was usually a comfort. It meant their own artillery was hitting the Germans. It meant help.

But then the shells began to arrive.

And they were landing in the wrong place.

The first explosion tore into the hillside where the Americans were dug in. Then another. Then another. The men looked up in confusion and then in dawning horror as they realized what was happening. The shells screaming down out of the sky were not German. They were American. Their own artillery, miles away, had made an error in the coordinates. The gunners believed they were hitting the German positions. Instead, they had walked their barrage directly onto the Lost Battalion.

Now picture the scene inside that pocket.

The men are already at the end of their strength. Starving, freezing, exhausted, surrounded. And now the sky itself opens up on them. High explosive shells burst among the foxholes, throwing up fountains of dirt and rock and shredded trees. Men who had survived four days of German attacks were now being blown apart by their own side. The wounded, lying helpless in the open, had no way to escape. Each explosion sent hot metal shrieking through the pocket.

And the worst part was this. The men on the ground knew it was a mistake. They knew that somewhere behind them, American gunners were working hard, believing they were helping, believing they were saving their comrades, when in fact every shell they fired was killing them. The barrage was an act of love turned into an act of slaughter, and there was no way to make it stop.

Major Whittlesey understood immediately what he had to do. He had to get a message to headquarters, right now, telling them to stop the barrage and giving them the exact position of his men so they would never make this mistake again. Every second of delay meant more of his men torn apart.

But how? The runners could not get through the German lines. The radio did not work. The telephone wires were cut.

There was only one way. The pigeons.

Whittlesey called for the birds.

He wrote out a message. Then he handed it to the pigeon handler, and the first bird was prepared and released into the sky above the pocket, carrying the plea to lift the barrage.

The men watched it rise.

And then they watched it die.

A crack of rifle fire, and the bird tumbled out of the sky. The Germans, seeing the pigeon rise, had shot it down before it could clear the treetops.

Whittlesey did not hesitate. He wrote the message again. A second bird was prepared. A second bird was released.

Every eye in the pocket followed it up into the air, willing it to fly, willing it to escape.

And a second time, the crack of rifle fire. The second bird spiraled down, dead.

Two pigeons gone. Two messages lost. And still the shells kept falling, still the men kept dying, and the situation was now beyond desperate.

There was one bird left.

One pigeon remained alive in the wicker basket, shaking with each nearby blast.

Its name was Cher Ami.

And everything now depended on it.
Let us slow down here, because this is the moment the whole story has been building toward. This is the moment two hundred lives hung on the wings of a single frightened bird.

The pigeon handler, a soldier who had cared for these birds through all the horror of the past days, reached into the basket and lifted Cher Ami out. Around them, the earth heaved with the impact of shells. Smoke drifted through the shattered trees. Men pressed themselves into the mud, trying to survive the storm of steel raining down from their own guns.

We are along the road parallel to two seven six point four. Our own artillery is dropping a barrage directly on us. For heaven's sake, stop it.

For heaven's sake, stop it.

That was the message. A location, so the gunners would know where the men were. And a plea. A raw, human cry, from a quiet lawyer who had watched his men suffer for four days and could not bear to watch them die by friendly fire.

The tiny slip of paper was rolled and slipped into the little metal capsule. The capsule was fixed to Cher Ami's leg. And then the handler carried the bird out to the open air, cupped it in his hands, and threw it up into the sky.

Cher Ami took to the air.

And then, to the horror of everyone watching, the bird did something that nearly stopped their hearts.

It did not fly away.

Instead, Cher Ami fluttered up into the branches of a nearby tree and perched there. As if confused. As if unwilling to leave. As if the noise and the smoke and the terror had overwhelmed even a bird bred for one purpose.

Think about what that felt like for the men below. Their last hope. Their final chance. And it was sitting in a tree, refusing to fly, while the shells kept falling and men kept dying all around them.

The handler and other soldiers began to shout and wave their arms, trying to frighten the bird into flight. Some accounts say they threw sticks and stones up toward the branches, anything to startle it into the air. Precious seconds ticked by. Each one felt like an eternity. Each one meant another explosion, another man wounded or killed.

And then, at last, Cher Ami spread its wings, lifted off the branch, and began to fly.

The men held their breath.

The bird climbed above the treetops, out into the open sky, in full view of the German riflemen who were waiting for exactly this. And now Cher Ami had to do what two birds before it had failed to do. It had to survive.

The Germans opened fire.
What happened next lasted only a matter of minutes. But those minutes decided the fate of two hundred men.

Cher Ami rose into the sky above the ravine, and immediately the German soldiers on the surrounding hills opened fire. Rifle rounds cracked through the air around the bird. Bullets that were meant to end this last desperate message tore past the small grey body climbing into the smoke.

And then one of them hit.

Cher Ami was struck in mid air.

Witnesses on the ground saw the bird stagger, saw it lurch and fall, and the sight must have crushed whatever hope remained in their chests. A bullet had ripped into the pigeon. It had been hit in the breast, torn through. One shot had struck it in the head or the eye, blinding it on one side. And the message capsule, fixed to its leg, was hanging on by almost nothing, because the leg itself had been nearly shot away, dangling now by a thin strand of tendon.

The bird began to drop.

Down toward the earth, down toward the German guns, down toward the same fate as the two pigeons before it.

And the men watching from their foxholes felt their last chance dying in the air in front of them.

But then, something happened that no one could have predicted. Something that people have marveled at for more than a century.

Cher Ami did not fall to the ground.

Somehow, out of some deep, stubborn instinct that no one fully understands, the wounded bird caught itself. It beat its wings. It steadied. And bleeding from its chest, blind in one eye, with one leg hanging by a thread and a message capsule swinging beneath it, Cher Ami began to fly again.

Not down. Up.

The bird pointed itself toward home, toward the loft miles behind the American lines, and it flew. It flew with a hole through its body. It flew half blind. It flew with a mangled leg and searing pain that we can only imagine. And it did not stop.

The distance it had to cover was roughly twenty five miles. Twenty five miles of open sky, over enemy held forest, in the state that this bird was in. By every reasonable expectation, Cher Ami should have died in the air and fallen into the trees, and the message should have been lost like the two before it.

But this bird refused to die.

For about twenty five minutes, Cher Ami flew. A wounded animal, driven by nothing but the ancient homing instinct bred into its bones, cutting through the sky toward the one place it knew, carrying a scrap of paper that meant life or death for hundreds of men.

And back in the pocket, the men could only wait, and pray, and endure the shells, with no way of knowing whether their message had gotten through, or whether their last bird lay dead somewhere in the forest.
Miles away, behind the American lines, sat a pigeon loft. It was the home base for the birds of the Signal Corps, the place every one of them was trained to return to. And then a battered grey shape appeared in the sky.

Cher Ami came home.

The bird descended toward the loft, and the soldiers there could see at once that something was terribly wrong with it. It was covered in blood. It was missing an eye. One leg was gone, shot away, leaving only a ragged stump, and hanging from that ruined leg by a last thread of tissue was the little metal message capsule.

The bird had flown roughly twenty five miles in about twenty five minutes, wounded almost to death, and it had brought the message home.

The soldiers carefully removed the capsule and unrolled the thin slip of paper inside. And they read Whittlesey's desperate words. The location of the Lost Battalion. The plea to stop the barrage. For heaven's sake, stop it.

The message shot up the chain of command. The coordinates were checked. The mistake was understood. And the order went out to the artillery batteries.

And the shells stopped falling on the Lost Battalion.

Somewhere in that muddy pocket in the Argonne, the men suddenly noticed the terrible thing that had been raining down on them come to an end. The explosions ceased. The friendly fire, the barrage that had been slaughtering them, went silent. And though they had no way of knowing exactly why, though they could not have known the extraordinary flight that had made it happen, the killing from their own guns was over.

A little grey bird had done it. A pigeon named Dear Friend, torn apart by bullets, had carried their cry for help across twenty five miles of sky and delivered it into the right hands.

But the men were not saved yet. The barrage had stopped, yes. But they were still trapped. Still surrounded. Still starving. Still cut off. Cher Ami had stopped their own guns from killing them, but the Germans were still all around, and the ordeal was not over.

The Lost Battalion had been trapped in that pocket since the second of October. By now they had endured days beyond counting, though it was in fact around five days of siege. Five days of hunger, thirst, cold, constant attack, and that terrible spell of friendly fire.

Of the roughly five hundred and fifty men who had gone into the forest, a great many were now dead or wounded. The survivors were skeletal, hollow eyed, filthy, and half mad with exhaustion. But they had not surrendered. Through everything, they had held their ground exactly where Major Whittlesey had told them to hold it.

And on the outside, the rest of the American army had been fighting like fury to reach them. Day after day, other units hurled themselves against the German lines, trying to break through and open a path to the trapped men. It was slow, bloody, brutal work. But the pressure was building, and the Germans, who were being pushed hard all along the front, could not hold the ring around the pocket forever.

Finally, on the evening of the seventh of October, relief broke through.

American forces at last punched a way through the German positions and reached the Lost Battalion. You can only imagine what that moment felt like for the men in the pocket. To see friendly faces coming toward them through the trees, not to attack, but to rescue. To know that the siege was over. That they had survived. That they were going home.

When the survivors were counted, the numbers told the story of what they had endured. Of the roughly five hundred and fifty men who had been trapped, only around one hundred and ninety four walked out of that pocket under their own power. The rest had been killed, wounded, or captured. It was a staggering price.

But the fact that any of them walked out at all was close to a miracle. And a crucial part of that miracle was the moment the friendly fire stopped. Because if that barrage had continued, if those American shells had kept falling on the pocket for hours more, historians and survivors alike agreed that many more men would have died. The end of that barrage saved lives. And the end of that barrage came because of Cher Ami.

That is why the story is told the way it is. That is why we say the pigeon saved two hundred soldiers. Because the men who walked out of that forest, and the families who got their sons and husbands and fathers back, owed a debt to a little grey bird that had flown through fire when two others had failed.

Major Whittlesey and his men became instant heroes back home. The newspapers seized on the story of the Lost Battalion. Whittlesey and two other officers received the Medal of Honor, the highest award for valor in the United States military. The men who survived were celebrated across the country.

But there was one hero of the Lost Battalion who could not read the newspapers, could not accept a medal, and did not even know what it had done.

The hero with feathers.
When Cher Ami landed at the loft, torn apart by German bullets, the bird should by all rights have died within minutes. The wounds were horrific. A bullet had passed through the breast. An eye was destroyed. A leg was all but severed, hanging by a thread.

But the same stubborn refusal to die that had carried the bird twenty five miles through the sky now kept it alive on the ground. And the soldiers of the Signal Corps, who understood exactly what this bird had just done, were determined to save it.

Army medics went to work on the pigeon as if it were a wounded soldier, which, in every way that mattered, it was. They cleaned and treated the wound in its chest. There was nothing to be done for the destroyed eye. And the ruined leg could not be saved, so it was carefully removed.

The men grew deeply attached to Cher Ami as it recovered. This was no longer just a message bird. This was a comrade who had done something extraordinary. As the pigeon healed, the soldiers reportedly carved it a tiny wooden leg, a small artificial limb to replace the one that had been shot away over the Argonne. Whether or not every detail of that wooden leg is exactly as the legend describes, the affection behind it was completely real. These hardened soldiers loved this little bird, and they cared for it with tenderness.

Cher Ami slowly recovered from wounds that should have been fatal. And the story of what the bird had done spread far and wide. The French were so moved by the pigeon's courage that they awarded Cher Ami one of their own military honors, the Croix de Guerre, a decoration given for bravery in combat. A medal for a pigeon. It sounds almost absurd, until you remember that this bird had flown through a hail of rifle fire, been shot nearly to pieces, and still delivered a message that saved the lives of nearly two hundred men.

When Cher Ami was well enough to travel, the bird was sent back to the United States, a national hero. There is a story that General John Pershing himself, the commander of all American forces in Europe, personally saw the bird off. Cher Ami sailed home across the same ocean the men of the Melting Pot division had crossed to go to war, returning now as one of the most celebrated animals in the world.

But the wounds Cher Ami had suffered were simply too severe to fully overcome. Less than a year after that legendary flight, in the middle of 1919, Cher Ami died from the lingering effects of the injuries received over the Argonne Forest.

The little bird that had refused to die in the sky finally succumbed to the wounds it had carried home.

And yet, even in death, the story of Cher Ami was not over.
When Cher Ami died, the Army did not simply bury the bird and forget it. They understood that this pigeon was a piece of history, a symbol of courage that deserved to be remembered.

So the body of Cher Ami was preserved. A taxidermist mounted the bird, and it was given to the Smithsonian Institution in Washington, one of the most famous collections of history and science in the entire world.

And that is where Cher Ami remains to this day.

More than one hundred years after that flight through the burning sky of the Argonne, you can go and stand in front of Cher Ami. The small grey body, one leg missing, is displayed as a treasure of American military history. Generations of visitors have paused in front of that little mounted bird, read the story, and tried to imagine the moment. The shells falling. The two dead pigeons. The last bird refusing to leave the tree. The bullets. The fall. And then the wings beating again, carrying a wounded animal home with two hundred lives tucked into a capsule on its shattered leg.

Cher Ami became one of the most famous animals of the entire First World War. The strange, humbling truth that in the middle of one of the most mechanized, industrial, dehumanizing wars in history, a war of gas and machine guns and artillery, the thing that saved hundreds of human lives was a small, living creature acting out of pure instinct and impossible endurance.

And there is one final mystery worth mentioning, because it lingers over the whole story.

For decades, Cher Ami was described as a hen, a female bird. That is how the story was told for generations. But much later, when experts examined the preserved body more closely, they came to believe that Cher Ami may in fact have been a male bird all along. Even now, the true nature of this famous pigeon is not entirely settled. The bird that carried so much certainty in that message, the exact location, the desperate plea, remains, in one small way, a puzzle.

But some things are not in doubt. Not the wounds. Not the flight. Not the two hundred men who lived because a little grey bird would not give up.
