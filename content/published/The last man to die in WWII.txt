On the morning of August the eighteenth, 1945, a twenty year old boy from Pennsylvania climbed into an airplane that had no reason to fly.

The war was over.

That is not an opinion. It is a fact you can check. Three days earlier, the Emperor of Japan had spoken to his people for the first time in history and told them the unthinkable. The guns were supposed to be cold. The killing was supposed to be finished. Across the world, sailors were kissing strangers in the street. Church bells were ringing in towns that had forgotten how to ring them.

And yet, at a scarred airfield on the island of Okinawa, four heavy bombers were being fuelled for a mission over Tokyo.
The boy carried a camera, not a gun. He played the trumpet. His mother still had his last letter on the kitchen table, unanswered.

By lunchtime, he would be bleeding to death in the belly of that bomber, thirty thousand feet above a country that had already surrendered.
He is remembered, when he is remembered at all, as the last man to die in World War Two.

The question that has haunted historians for eighty years is not how he died.

It is why anyone was still shooting.
To understand why a boy died after the war ended, you have to understand that the war did not end the way the history books make it sound. There was no switch. There was no moment when every rifle in the world went quiet at once. What happened instead was messier, stranger, and far more dangerous.

It began with a phonograph record.

By the second week of August 1945, Japan was a country on fire. Two atomic bombs had incinerated Hiroshima and Nagasaki. The Soviet Union had torn into Manchuria. Tokyo itself had been burned to charcoal months earlier by firebombing raids that killed more people in a single night than either atomic bomb. The Imperial cabinet was deadlocked, split between men who wanted to accept the Allied terms and men who wanted the entire nation to die on its feet.

And so, in the small hours of the fourteenth of August, Emperor Hirohito did something no Japanese emperor had ever done. He broke the tie himself. He announced that Japan would accept the Potsdam Declaration. He would tell the nation directly, in his own voice, through the radio.
Here is the detail that changes everything. The Emperor did not go on the air live. His speech was recorded onto two fragile phonograph discs in a room inside the Imperial Palace, to be broadcast at noon the following day.

Two discs. That is all. Two brittle records, and the surrender of an empire pressed into their grooves.

The Emperor's own people had never heard his voice. To most Japanese, he was not merely a monarch. He was a living deity, a figure so far above ordinary life that hearing him speak was almost blasphemy. When those records left the Palace, they were the most valuable objects on the planet.

And there were men in Tokyo that night who were willing to kill everyone in the building to make sure the world never heard them.
They were young officers of the War Ministry. Their leader was a major named Kenji Hatanaka. He was not a monster. He was something far more frightening. He was a true believer, a man so certain that surrender was a national disgrace that he genuinely believed the Emperor had been tricked, kidnapped in spirit by cowardly advisers.

That night, Hatanaka and his conspirators launched a coup.

They forged orders. They seized control of the palace grounds with troops from the Imperial Guard. When the commander of the Guards Division, General Takeshi Mori, refused to join them, Hatanaka shot him dead in his own office. Then the rebels went hunting through the palace corridors, room by room, in the dark, searching for two small phonograph records.
Picture it. The most important recording in human history, hidden somewhere in a warren of offices, while men with pistols kick down doors and telephone lines are cut and Tokyo lies blacked out under the threat of another firebombing raid.

They tore the place apart for hours. They never found them.

The records had been hidden in a small safe in a room used by a court chamberlain, buried under a pile of documents, in a section of the palace the rebels overlooked. By dawn, the coup had collapsed. The commander of the Eastern District Army refused to support it. Hatanaka, realising it was finished, went out to the plaza in front of the Imperial Palace and shot himself in the forehead.

At noon on the fifteenth of August, the records were smuggled out in a laundry basket, driven across a broken city, and played on the air.
The nation heard a thin, formal, archaic voice explain that the war situation had developed not necessarily to Japan's advantage. It was so heavy with courtly language that many listeners were not certain what they had heard. But they understood the shape of it. They understood that the impossible had happened.

Most of Japan wept, and obeyed.

Most.
Because the coup in the palace failed. But it was never the only one.


Twenty miles southwest of Tokyo lay an airfield called Atsugi. It was home to the 302nd Naval Air Group, and its commander was a man named Yasuna Kozono.

Kozono was a brilliant, difficult, and dangerous officer. He was an innovator, a man who had helped pioneer night fighting tactics that tore into American bombers. He was also, in that August, running a fever from malaria, and something in him had come loose.

When the surrender broadcast ended, Kozono gathered his men on the airfield and told them that the Emperor's message was not the Emperor's will. He told them that surrender was treason. He told them that the sacred soil of Japan had never been invaded in three thousand years and would not be invaded now. He announced that his air group would fight on, alone, without orders, without a government, until every one of them was dead.
His men flew over Tokyo and dropped leaflets urging the population to rise up and reject the surrender. Other rebel units did the same. For several days in the middle of August 1945, the sky over the capital of Japan belonged to no one at all.

This is the part that gets edited out of the tidy version of history. Between the fifteenth of August and the formal signing on the second of September, Japan was not a defeated nation waiting politely for the occupation. It was a nation with a functioning military, thousands of intact aircraft, millions of men under arms, and an unknown number of officers who considered the surrender an obscenity and were prepared to die proving it.

Eventually the authorities dealt with Kozono. He was declared insane, forcibly removed, and hospitalised. Loyalist officers moved onto the airfields and began the extraordinary work of persuading men to hand over their weapons. In the end, the surrender held. The propellers were removed from aircraft across the country so that no hothead could take off.

But that took time.

And in the days it took, the Americans had a problem of their own.
Here is the impossible situation that lands on the desk of General Douglas MacArthur in the third week of August 1945.

In roughly two weeks, he intends to fly into Japan, land at that very airfield at Atsugi, and take control of a nation of seventy million people who have never been defeated in their history, using an initial force so small it could be wiped out in an afternoon. He is going to walk unarmed into the home islands and simply behave as though he owns them.

It is one of the greatest bluffs ever attempted. And a bluff requires information.

MacArthur has to know if Japan is actually complying. Are the airfields being disarmed? Are the propellers really coming off? Are the coastal guns still manned? Is there a rebel air group out there waiting to shoot the occupation force out of the sky, or worse, waiting to kill the general himself the moment his transport touches down?

There is only one way to find out. Somebody has to go and look.
The order goes out for photographic reconnaissance missions over Tokyo. Fly over the enemy capital. Photograph the airfields. Photograph the harbours. Confirm the surrender with your own eyes.

And then comes the order that everything else turns on, the order that will kill a boy from Pennsylvania.

Do not fire unless fired upon.

The crews are told, in effect, that they are not at war anymore. They are observers. They are to be seen, and they are to see. They are to provoke nothing.

Which means, if a Japanese fighter climbs up to meet them, they must let it come.
The aircraft chosen for those missions was one of the strangest machines of the entire war. It was called the B32 Dominator, and almost nobody has heard of it, which is exactly the point.

The Dominator was built by Consolidated as an insurance policy. If the Superfortress, the famous bomber that carried the atomic bombs, failed to work, the Dominator would take its place. The Superfortress did not fail. So the Dominator became a bomber without a purpose, a magnificent, enormous, temperamental orphan of a machine.

Fewer than one hundred and twenty were ever completed. They arrived in the Pacific in the last weeks of the war, in tiny numbers, for what the Air Force delicately called combat testing. They flew from a battered strip on Okinawa, a place still littered with the debris of the bloodiest battle of the Pacific.

They were plagued with problems. Engines overheated. Turrets jammed. The nose wheel had a habit of collapsing. The men who flew them joked, in the way that men joke when the alternative is thinking too hard, that the Dominator was a coffin with four engines.

And now these unloved aircraft, crewed by men who all believed the war was finished, were the ones being sent to fly straight down the throat of Tokyo.
Their bomb bays were empty. Their cameras were full.

On the seventeenth of August, two of them took off and ran the first mission over the Tokyo area. It should have been a milk run, a sightseeing tour over a beaten enemy.

It was not.

Japanese fighters came up.

They came up out of Atsugi and the naval air stations around the bay, the same rebel airfields, the same furious men. They circled. They probed. They made passes at the big American bombers, and some of them opened fire, and the American gunners, under orders, mostly held their fire and simply took photographs of the aircraft trying to kill them.
One Dominator came home with damage. Nobody was killed. The crews landed on Okinawa shaken, furious, and confused, and they said what any sane man would say.

The war is over. Why is anyone shooting at us?

They filed their reports. They handed in their film. And that night the order came down from headquarters for the mission to be repeated the following morning.
Think about that for a moment, because this is the hinge of the whole story.

These men now knew, with absolute certainty, that Japanese fighters were still hunting over Tokyo. They knew the enemy had not fully stood down. They knew their own guns were effectively leashed by an order not to provoke a fight.

And they were told to go back. Tomorrow. Same place. Same time.

Some of them barely slept.

One of the men on that list was a twenty year old sergeant named Anthony James Marchione.
Anthony Marchione was born in Pottstown, Pennsylvania, the son of Italian immigrants. His father worked hard, his mother kept the house, and the family was the ordinary, decent, unremarkable kind of American family that produced most of the men who won that war and got almost none of the credit for it.

Tony was slight, dark eyed, and quiet. He was known in his neighbourhood for two things.

The first was that he was kind. The kind of boy who carried groceries for the widow down the street without being asked.

The second was the trumpet.
He played in the local band. He loved the big swing sound of the era, the sound coming out of the radio every night, and there was a version of his life, a life that was perfectly possible, in which he came home from the war and played in dance halls on Saturday nights and married a local girl and grew old and told his grandchildren almost nothing about any of this.

He enlisted. He trained as an aerial photographer, which meant he was not a gunner, not a pilot, not a bombardier. His job was to lie in the belly of an aircraft with a camera and record what was below. In an emergency he could man a gun. But his weapon was a lens.

He arrived in the Pacific in the last stretch of the war and flew a handful of missions. He was young enough that he still wrote home constantly. In his letters he told his mother and his sister not to worry, because everyone knew it was nearly finished.

He was right. It was nearly finished.
On the fifteenth of August, when the news of the Japanese surrender reached Okinawa, the island erupted. Men fired flares into the sky. They fired their weapons in the air, and men actually died from the falling bullets, killed by their own side's celebration. They wept and drank and screamed at the sky, because it meant the invasion of Japan was cancelled, and every man there had done the arithmetic on what an invasion of the home islands would have cost, and every man there had privately concluded that he was going to die in it.

They had been reprieved. All of them.

Three days later, Anthony Marchione put on his flight gear and climbed aboard a B32 Dominator for a photographic mission over Tokyo.
Four aircraft were scheduled. Two dropped out with mechanical faults, which was entirely typical of the Dominator. Two continued.

Marchione was aboard the second aircraft, the one without a name painted on its nose, a machine known only by its number. It carried a crew of thirteen. The lead aircraft, flying ahead, carried a name that has become the only reason most people ever hear of this story at all.

Hobo Queen the Second.

They climbed out over the ocean, turned north, and flew towards the capital of an empire that had surrendered three days earlier.

The cameras were running. The guns were manned. And the order stood.

Do not fire unless fired upon.
They reached the coast in the early afternoon. Below them, Tokyo Bay opened up, grey and vast, the shattered city smeared along its edge, the burned districts still black from the firebombing, the harbour full of ships that would never sail.

The two Dominators went in at about twenty thousand feet.
In the belly of the second aircraft, Anthony Marchione was working with the photographic team. Beside him was a photographer named Joseph Lacharite. Their job was simple and completely absorbing. Track the target. Hold the frame. Get the image.

They photographed the airfields. They photographed the naval installations. It was going exactly as briefed.

And then, somewhere below them, on the airfields that had refused to surrender, engines began to start.
The Japanese fighters came up in numbers. Not one or two. More than a dozen. They came from the naval air groups around the bay, from Atsugi and its neighbours, and among the pilots climbing towards those two American bombers were some of the most dangerous men alive, including veterans who had been fighting since the beginning, men with dozens of victories, men who had watched every friend they ever had die.

Understand what is in the mind of a pilot climbing into that sky.

His country has surrendered. His Emperor has spoken. His war is lost. Every promise he was made has turned out to be a lie. And overhead, unmistakable and arrogant, an enemy bomber is casually photographing his ruined homeland.

Whatever the correct word is for what those men felt, it was not restraint.
The fighters attacked.

They came in fast, from above and behind, hammering at the two Dominators. The bombers were huge, slow, and carrying no bombs, only film. Their gun turrets were unreliable. Some jammed almost immediately. The crews found themselves in a running battle in a machine that had been in service for a matter of weeks, against an enemy that officially no longer existed.

The Hobo Queen took hits. The second aircraft, Marchione's aircraft, took worse.

Cannon shells and machine gun fire raked the fuselage. In the photographic compartment, Joseph Lacharite was hit in the legs and went down hard, bleeding, unable to move.
And here is the moment. Here is why this story matters and why the name of Anthony Marchione should not be forgotten.

He did not have to do what he did next. There was nothing in his training, nothing in his orders, that required a twenty year old camera operator to crawl through a burst of enemy cannon fire to reach a wounded man.

He went to him anyway.

Marchione moved to help Lacharite, to pull him clear, to stop the bleeding. He was doing exactly what the best of us do, which is to move towards the person who is hurt, at the exact moment when every rational instinct in the body is screaming at you to get low and stay still.
Another burst hit the aircraft.

Anthony Marchione was struck in the chest.
The crew got to him within seconds. They pulled him back from the open hatch. They pressed their hands into the wound. They tore open first aid kits and shouted at him and held him while the aircraft twisted through the sky and the fighters kept coming and the pilot fought to break away and run for the sea.

He was conscious for a little while. He did not scream. The men who were with him remembered that he stayed calm, and that he was cold, and that he asked, quietly, whether he was going to be alright.

He bled to death in the arms of his crewmates roughly half an hour later, somewhere over the coast of Japan, on the eighteenth of August, 1945.
The two Dominators escaped and made it back to Okinawa. The damaged aircraft came down with a dead boy and a badly wounded man aboard, and the crew climbed out onto the coral and stood there in the sunshine of a world at peace.

The war was over. It had been over for three days.

They had just lost a man.
In Pottstown, Pennsylvania, the Marchione family had spent the past three days celebrating. Their boy was coming home. The whole street knew it. The war was finished, and the impossible had happened, and Tony had made it.

Then the telegram arrived.

Try to hold that in your mind for a second, because it is almost too cruel to be real. Every mother in America who lost a son in that war lost him in a war. Emelia Marchione lost hers in the peace.
Two weeks later, on the second of September, 1945, Japanese officials stood on the deck of an American battleship in Tokyo Bay and signed the instrument of surrender, and the newsreels rolled, and that is the image the world kept. That is the ending everybody remembers. Clean, formal, and final.

Anthony Marchione is not in that picture. Neither is the story of what happened over Tokyo on the eighteenth of August.

The mission was quietly buried. In the fragile, dangerous weeks of that first occupation, when the whole enterprise depended on the idea that the Japanese were complying and the Americans were magnanimous, nobody in authority had any appetite for a story about American airmen being shot to pieces by Japanese fighters after the surrender. It complicated things. It was inconvenient.

So the B32 Dominator was scrapped, all of it, within months. Hundreds of airframes were flown straight from the factory to the desert and cut up for aluminium. It became one of the only major American combat aircraft of the war of which not a single complete example survives today.

The bomber vanished. And the story of the boy who died in it went with it.
He was buried at home. His family kept his trumpet.

For decades, hardly anyone outside Pottstown knew what had happened, and even now, when documentaries list the last casualties of the Second World War, the argument goes back and forth.

There was Charles Havlat, an American private of Czech descent, cut down by German fire in Czechoslovakia on the seventh of May, 1945, in the last minutes before the ceasefire took hold in Europe, killed within sight of his ancestors' homeland. His unit received the ceasefire order minutes after his death.

There were men who died in prison camps that week. There were men killed by mines and accidents and untreated wounds long after the guns went quiet. In truth, the war did not have a last man. It had a long, ragged tail of them, and it kept killing for years, and in some families it never really stopped.

But if you are looking for the last man killed in combat, in the air, by an enemy who was still shooting at him after his country and theirs had both agreed to stop, then the answer is a twenty year old aerial photographer from Pennsylvania who crawled towards a wounded friend when he did not have to.
I keep coming back to one thing.

He knew.

That is what will not leave me alone. Anthony Marchione knew the war was over. He had celebrated it three days earlier along with everybody else. He knew, when he climbed into that aircraft on the morning of the eighteenth of August, that he was flying over a country that had already surrendered, on a mission with no bombs, for photographs that most of the world would never see.

And when the shooting started and a man went down beside him, he moved towards the wounded man.

Not for victory. There was nothing left to win. Not for his country. His country was already safe. He did it for the person who was bleeding next to him, which is, in the end, the only reason anybody has ever done anything brave.
The bombers were melted down. The airfields were bulldozed. The rebel pilots grew old and were reabsorbed into a peaceful country that would never fight another war. The generals got their memoirs and their statues and their ticker tape.

And a boy from Pottstown, who was supposed to come home and play trumpet in a dance band, became a footnote in a story that everybody thinks they already know.

The war ended on the second of September, 1945.

For Anthony Marchione, it ended on the eighteenth of August, thirty thousand feet above Tokyo, with a camera in his hands and a friend to reach.

Say his name out loud once, if you can. It costs nothing, and it is roughly eighty years overdue.
