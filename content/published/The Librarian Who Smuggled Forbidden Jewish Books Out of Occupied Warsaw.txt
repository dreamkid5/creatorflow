To understand why this story matters so deeply, we first need to go back.

Back to a time when Warsaw was not a city of terror.

Before the war, Warsaw, Poland was one of the most vibrant Jewish cities in the entire world.

Think about that for a moment.

More than 350,000 Jewish people lived in Warsaw by 1939. That was roughly one third of the entire city's population.

And these were not just people passing through.

They had lived there for generations. Centuries. Their families had built businesses, hospitals, schools, and synagogues there. Their grandparents were buried in the soil of Warsaw. Their names were carved into the stones of that city.

Warsaw's Jewish community had its own newspapers, its own theater scene, its own music, its own literature.

And its own libraries.
Among the most important of these was the Main Jewish Library in Warsaw.

Officially, it was known as the library of the Grosser Foundation, named after the Bundist leader Bronisław Grosser. But everyone in the community simply called it the Jewish Public Library.

It sat on Tłomackie Street, just steps away from the Great Synagogue of Warsaw. That synagogue was one of the most beautiful buildings in all of Poland.

The library held over 100,000 books and manuscripts.

Can you imagine that?

One hundred thousand books in Hebrew, Yiddish, Polish, and Russian. Books of prayer and science and law and story. Books written by some of the greatest minds in Jewish history. Books that had survived pogroms and poverty and centuries of persecution.

They were safe there. Loved there. Read there.

And working in that library was a woman named Gustawa Lewinówna.
Gustawa had worked with books her entire adult life. She was a trained librarian, organized and meticulous. She knew those shelves better than she knew her own home.

She knew which books made children laugh and which ones made scholars cry. She knew the smell of the leather-bound volumes from the 1800s and the crisp new paper smell of books that had just arrived from the printers.

She loved them. All of them.

And so did the other librarians and scholars who worked alongside her.

People like Emanuel Ringelblum, a young historian who believed deeply that documenting Jewish life in Poland was sacred work.

People like Noah Prylucki, a linguist and politician who had dedicated his life to the study and celebration of the Yiddish language.

They were scholars. Dreamers. Book people.

None of them could have imagined what was coming.
On September 1st, 1939, Germany invaded Poland.

And in 17 days, Warsaw was under siege.

The world they knew, the world of books and reading rooms and quiet scholarship, was about to be destroyed.
Now, here is something that history does not always emphasize enough.

The Nazis did not just want to kill Jewish people.

They wanted to erase them from history entirely.

And one of the ways they planned to do that was by destroying Jewish books, culture, art, music, and memory.

This was not a new idea in 1939. It had started years earlier.

On the night of May 10th, 1933, six years before the invasion of Poland, Nazi student organizations across Germany built giant bonfires in town squares.

And into those fires, they threw books.

Thousands and thousands of books.

They threw in the works of Jewish writers like Heinrich Heine, Sigmund Freud, and Albert Einstein. They threw in books by anyone who disagreed with Nazi ideology.

It was a spectacle. A performance of hatred.

And the people in those crowds cheered.

But here is something important to remember about that night. About Heinrich Heine specifically.

A century before the Nazis came to power, Heinrich Heine himself had written a line that now seemed horribly prophetic.

He wrote that where books are burned, in the end people are also burned.

He wrote that in 1820. More than a hundred years before Hitler.

History had given a warning. And the world had not listened.
When the Nazis occupied Warsaw in late September 1939, they brought that same burning philosophy with them.

Within weeks of the occupation, new rules began to appear.

Jewish people were required to wear white armbands with a blue Star of David. They could not ride public transportation. They could not visit parks. They could not attend theaters or concerts or museums.

And their books and libraries were targeted almost immediately.

Nazi officials began raiding Jewish institutions, confiscating collections, and destroying what they deemed to be dangerous material.
Officers would walk into a Jewish home, see a bookshelf full of Hebrew or Yiddish texts, and simply take them. Or burn them on the spot.

Entire libraries were looted. Some books were shipped back to Germany to be studied by Nazi scholars who wanted to understand, and then eliminate, Jewish culture. Other books were simply destroyed.

The message was clear.

You do not exist. Your history does not exist. Your language does not exist. Your God does not exist.

You are nothing.

Now, for an ordinary person, this was terrifying. Heartbreaking. Paralyzing.

But for a librarian, someone whose entire life was devoted to the power and permanence of books, it was something even more.

It was a call to action.
Gustawa Lewinówna looked at those empty shelves and made a choice.

She was going to fight back.

Not with a gun. Not with a bomb. Not with a march or a protest.

With books.
By October 1940, one year into the Nazi occupation, the German authorities announced something that would change everything.

They were going to create a ghetto.

A sealed-off section of Warsaw, surrounded by walls, where all Jewish residents of the city would be forced to live.

The order came down and the Jewish people of Warsaw had very little time to gather their belongings and move inside the designated area.

Over 400,000 Jewish people were eventually crammed into an area of about 1.3 square miles. That is smaller than many shopping mall complexes today.
Think about what that means.

You have lived in your apartment for 20 years. Your children were born there. You know every neighbor on your block. And suddenly, an officer comes to your door and tells you to leave by a certain date or face the consequences.

You can take what you can carry.

Your furniture stays. Your neighbors' homes stay. Your whole life stays.

And you walk behind a brick wall that is three meters high and topped with barbed wire.

The wall was 11 miles long around the perimeter. It was guarded by Nazi soldiers on one side and Polish police on the other. Getting in or out without permission was punishable by death.

That is not a figure of speech.

If you were caught leaving the ghetto without authorization, you were shot on the spot.

The Nazis were deliberately starving the ghetto. Food rations were set at starvation levels. Disease spread rapidly because of the overcrowding. People died in the streets every single day.

And yet, remarkably, astonishingly, even inside those walls, people kept reading.
Inside the ghetto, an underground culture of survival sprang up almost immediately.

Secret schools were organized for children whose education had been officially banned. Newspapers were printed and passed hand to hand. Concerts were performed in apartment living rooms. Theater groups put on plays.

And libraries operated.

Yes. Libraries.

Even inside the ghetto, people maintained small lending libraries, circulating books among residents who desperately needed something to hold onto besides despair.

Reading was not an escape from reality.

It was a form of resistance to reality.

To open a book was to say: You have not broken me. You have not erased me. I still think. I still feel. I still exist.
This was the world that Gustawa Lewinówna was trying to protect.

But there was a problem.

The most valuable books, the irreplaceable manuscripts, the ancient texts, the rare and precious collections that the community had built over generations, many of them were still in the library on Tłomackie Street.

And Tłomackie Street was on the non-Jewish, or Aryan, side of the new wall.

Outside the ghetto.

The Nazis had drawn the boundary carefully. And the Jewish library was left on the outside.

That meant the books were vulnerable. Unprotected. And it was only a matter of time before the Nazis would come for them.

Gustawa and her colleagues knew this.

And so they began to plan.
The plan was simple in concept and almost impossibly dangerous in practice.

They would smuggle the books.

They would move as many of the most valuable books and manuscripts as humanly possible from the library on Tłomackie Street into the ghetto, where the community could protect them and keep them alive.

Think about what that required.

First, you had to carry books out of a building without Nazi authorities noticing. The library was technically closed, but it was still being monitored by German officials who wanted to know exactly what was there.

Second, you had to physically get those books through or around the ghetto wall. A wall that was patrolled by armed guards. Where being caught with the wrong thing at the wrong time could mean instant death.

Third, you had to find a safe place to store them inside the ghetto, where German soldiers regularly conducted raids and searches.

And fourth, you had to do all of this in complete secrecy, because any one person who knew about the plan and chose to inform the Nazis could end everything in a moment.
Now, I want you to really sit with that for a moment.

These were not trained spies. They were not members of a military resistance organization. Most of them were librarians, teachers, and scholars.

People who had spent their lives in quiet reading rooms with card catalogs and lending ledgers.

And here they were, planning an operation that would require the nerves of a soldier and the cunning of a smuggler.

How did they do it?
The first method was hiding books inside ordinary objects.

Books were wrapped in brown paper and placed inside shopping bags and baskets, the kind that anyone might carry on a trip to the market. They were tucked inside laundry bundles. Slipped into the false bottoms of luggage.

Heavy manuscripts were hidden inside large loaves of bread. No, really. Bakers in the ghetto would help by baking enormous round loaves with hollow centers just large enough to hold a folded bundle of pages.

Small children were used to carry books past checkpoints, because guards were less likely to thoroughly search a child's school bag or coat pocket.

Rolls of film containing photographs of rare manuscripts were hidden inside clothing.

Some books were passed through holes in the ghetto wall at night, handed from person to person in the dark, with guards just meters away.
But the most daring method involved the everyday flow of goods in and out of the ghetto.

Because the ghetto wall had checkpoints. And through those checkpoints, certain things were allowed to move with proper permits.

Food was brought in through those checkpoints, though never enough. Goods produced by Jewish workers in ghetto factories were taken out.

People who had the right papers, workers with special passes, could sometimes move through.

And certain trusted individuals on the Aryan side could make contact with people on the ghetto side and pass things through the checkpoint system.

Gustawa Lewinówna was on the outside, on the Aryan side of the wall. This meant she had freedom of movement that the people trapped inside did not.

She became a courier. A bridge between two worlds.

She would carry books to the checkpoint. Pass them to contacts on the inside. Or arrange for trusted intermediaries to make the transfer.

Every single time she did this, she risked her life.

Not just because she was helping Jewish people, which was itself illegal and punishable by death under Nazi rule.

But because she was preserving Jewish culture. And that was something the Nazis viewed as an act of war against everything they stood for.
There is a particular image I want you to hold in your mind.

It is a cold Warsaw night in the winter of 1940 or 1941.

Gustawa is walking through the streets of the city with a bag over her shoulder. Inside that bag, wrapped in cloth and tucked beneath some ordinary groceries, is a manuscript that might be hundreds of years old.

She walks at a normal pace. Not too fast. Not too slow. Nothing that would draw attention.

She keeps her face calm.

But inside her chest, her heart is pounding.

Because there are soldiers on the street corners. Because anyone might report her. Because one wrong moment, one bad stroke of luck, one guard who decides to search her bag more carefully, and everything is over.

And still she walks.

Because she has decided that these books matter more than her fear.
Now, the story of the smuggled books cannot be told without telling the story of a parallel operation happening simultaneously inside the ghetto walls.

An operation that was, in its own way, even more extraordinary.

It was called Oyneg Shabes.

In Yiddish, those words mean "Joy of the Sabbath."

And it was one of the most ambitious and heartbreaking historical projects ever attempted in the history of humanity.

The man who created it was Emanuel Ringelblum.

Emanuel Ringelblum was a historian. A brilliant one. A man who had dedicated his career to documenting the lives of ordinary Jewish people in Poland, not just the great rabbis and wealthy merchants, but the butchers, the seamstresses, the schoolteachers, and the children.

He believed that every ordinary life contained extraordinary history.

And when the Nazis sealed the Warsaw Ghetto in November 1940, Ringelblum understood something with terrifying clarity.

He understood that the Nazis intended to destroy not just the people of the ghetto but the evidence that they had ever existed.

And he made a decision.

He would not let them.
Ringelblum organized a secret group of about 60 scholars, writers, teachers, rabbis, social workers, and ordinary people inside the ghetto.

They met in secret on Saturday afternoons, on the Sabbath, which is why they used the Hebrew phrase Oyneg Shabes as their code name.

And together, they did something that defies easy description.

They documented everything.

They wrote diaries. They conducted interviews. They gathered testimonies from refugees who had fled from other cities already destroyed by the Nazis. They collected statistics. They saved photographs and postcards. They recorded the prices of food in the ghetto so that future historians would know exactly how impossible survival was. They documented every German decree and order. They wrote about the deportations.

They collected thousands and thousands of pages.

Letters from children describing what they saw. Reports from doctors about the diseases spreading through overcrowded apartments. Testimonies from survivors of the terrible killings already happening in the east. Songs. Poems. Jokes. Even jokes, because they believed that even the humor people used to survive deserved to be remembered.

They were not just documenting history.

They were arguing with the future.

They were saying: We know you may not survive to tell the world what happened here. We know the Nazis may win. But if anyone ever digs up this ground in the years to come, they will find the truth. Not the story the Nazis tell. Our truth. The truth of what it was like to be human in a time of monsters.
And here is where the story of Gustawa Lewinówna and the story of Oyneg Shabes come together.

Because both projects shared the same urgent mission.

Preserve the evidence of Jewish life so that it cannot be erased.

Books that Gustawa smuggled into the ghetto were sometimes given to Ringelblum's network to be documented and protected.

Testimonies and writings collected by the Oyneg Shabes group were sometimes smuggled back out of the ghetto by Gustawa and her contacts, to be kept safe on the Aryan side.

They were two halves of the same act of defiance.
Now here is something remarkable that I want you to know.

Ringelblum and his group knew that the situation was becoming hopeless by 1942.

Mass deportations from the ghetto to the Treblinka death camp had begun.

Hundreds of thousands of people were being taken away and killed.

The Oyneg Shabes group realized they were running out of time.

And so they did something extraordinary.

They packed all of their collected documents, all those thousands of pages of testimony and history and evidence, into metal boxes and large milk canisters.

And they buried them.

They buried them in the basements and courtyards of buildings inside the ghetto.

In the ground beneath the city that was being destroyed above them.

And their hope was simple: that someday, someone would find these buried treasures. That someone would dig them up and show the world what had happened here.

That the truth would survive, even if the people could not.

Emanuel Ringelblum himself was eventually captured and killed by the Nazis in March 1944. He died with his wife and young son, right there in Warsaw.

But his archive survived.

After the war, two of the three buried caches were found. Thousands of pages, pulled from the earth. Ringelblum's final act of defiance had worked.
In May of 1943, after the heroic but ultimately overwhelmed Warsaw Ghetto Uprising, in which Jewish fighters armed with almost nothing held off the Nazi army for nearly a month, the Nazi commander Heinrich Himmler gave an order.

He wanted a symbolic end to the Warsaw Ghetto.

And so, on the 16th of May, 1943, he personally detonated the explosives that brought down the Great Synagogue of Warsaw.

The same synagogue that had stood just steps from the library where Gustawa had spent her life.

The same synagogue whose soaring towers had looked out over Tłomackie Street and over the books and the readers and the scholars who had come through those library doors for generations.

The synagogue fell.

And Himmler reportedly looked at the dust rising over the ruins and said, with deep satisfaction, "The Jewish quarter of Warsaw is no more."

He was wrong.

He did not know about the books already moved to safety.

He did not know about the buried boxes.

He did not know about the photographs and diaries and testimonies hidden in places he would never think to look.

He did not know about the quiet woman who had walked through his checkpoints with her shopping bag full of history.

And he did not know about the network of people, both Jewish and non-Jewish, who had risked everything not with weapons but with words, paper, and ink.
The Nazis wanted to write the story.

But the librarians had already stolen the pen.
When the war finally ended in 1945, Warsaw was a ruin.

Nearly 90 percent of the city had been destroyed. The ghetto itself was a field of rubble and ash.

But the books that had been hidden, the manuscripts that had been smuggled, the documents that had been buried in the earth, some of them had survived.

Not all of them. The losses were staggering and heartbreaking.

But some.

And some is the most important word in history.
Collections that Gustawa Lewinówna and her colleagues had saved were eventually reunited with surviving members of the Jewish community and later housed in institutions that would protect them permanently.

The two recovered caches of the Oyneg Shabes archive, found in 1946 and 1950, contained tens of thousands of documents. They are now held at the Jewish Historical Institute in Warsaw, where scholars from around the world continue to study them to this day.

The third cache has never been found. Somewhere beneath the streets of modern Warsaw, it may still be waiting.

Books that were smuggled out of Nazi-occupied Europe ended up in libraries in Israel, in the United States, and in other countries where they became the seed of new collections, new scholarship, and new memory.

The YIVO Institute for Jewish Research in New York, which had its headquarters in Vilna, Lithuania, lost most of its collection to Nazi looting. But dedicated scholars, including a group nicknamed the Paper Brigade, secretly saved thousands of books and documents by smuggling them out of Nazi control. Many of those books eventually made it to New York, where YIVO still stands today as one of the premier institutions for the study of Jewish history and culture.
And what about Gustawa Lewinówna?

The historical record about her individual fate is, as is true of many ordinary heroes of the Holocaust, incomplete.

This is one of the great tragedies of that era. So many people who did extraordinary things lived and died without being fully documented or remembered. Their stories were often lost in the same catastrophe they were fighting against.

What we know is that she was part of a network of brave people, both Jewish and non-Jewish Polish citizens, who understood that culture and memory are not small things.

They are not extras. They are not luxuries.

They are the very substance of what it means to be human.

And if you allow a culture's memory to be destroyed, you allow a part of that people's soul to be destroyed along with it.

Gustawa and her colleagues refused to allow that.

They paid enormous personal prices. Some were caught. Some were killed. Some survived the war but lived with the weight of what they had witnessed for the rest of their lives.

But the books they saved are still here.
So why am I telling you this story today?

Why does it matter in 2024, more than 80 years after those events?

I want to give you three reasons.

The first reason is this.

The Nazis understood something that we sometimes forget.

They understood that to destroy a people, you first destroy their stories.

You burn their books. You silence their language. You erase their history. You make them invisible. And once they are invisible, it becomes easier to treat them as less than human.

This is not ancient history. This pattern has repeated itself across many cultures and many conflicts throughout the 20th century and beyond.

Anytime you see a group of people trying to ban books, erase history from school curriculums, destroy cultural institutions, or declare certain kinds of knowledge dangerous, you are seeing the first steps of something that history has taught us to recognize.

Pay attention.

Gustawa Lewinówna and Emanuel Ringelblum and the hundreds of other ordinary people in this story were not heroes at birth.
